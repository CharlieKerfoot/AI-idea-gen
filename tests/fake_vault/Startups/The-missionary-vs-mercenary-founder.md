# The Missionary vs Mercenary Founder

John Doerr's distinction between "missionary" and "mercenary" founders has become a cliche in venture capital. Missionaries build because they believe in a vision; mercenaries build to make money. VCs claim to prefer missionaries, because missionaries persevere through the inevitable hard times that would make a mercenary quit.

But the distinction is less clean than it appears. Most successful founders are both — they believe in their vision and they want to get rich. The interesting question is not which type is better, but what happens when the missionary and mercenary impulses conflict.

The conflict emerges at predictable moments. Should you take the acqui-hire offer that makes you personally wealthy but kills the product? Should you pivot to enterprise sales because that is where the revenue is, even though your vision was consumer? Should you raise a round at a valuation that will make future fundraising nearly impossible if you miss your targets? In each case, the missionary says no and the mercenary says yes.

[[Software-eating-the-world-revisited|The history of software]] suggests that mercenaries often produce better outcomes for the world, even if their products are less inspiring. Enterprise software is boring and lucrative. Consumer apps are exciting and mostly fail. The missionary founder who insists on building the beautiful consumer product while the mercenary builds the ugly but profitable B2B tool may have more integrity, but the mercenary's customers are actually getting value.

The deepest problem with the missionary frame is that it is susceptible to [[Incentive-blindness|incentive blindness]]. The founder who believes she is a missionary has a harder time recognizing when her "vision" has become a rationalization for sunk costs. The mercenary, precisely because he is honest about his motivations, can pivot more easily. He has nothing to prove. [[Free-will-as-useful-fiction|The fiction of pure motivation]] may be useful for recruiting, but it is dangerous for decision-making.
