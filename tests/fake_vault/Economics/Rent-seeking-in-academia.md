# Rent-Seeking in Academia

Academic publishing is one of the most successful rent-seeking operations in modern capitalism, and it persists because the rent-seekers have convinced their victims that the arrangement is natural and meritocratic.

Here is the deal: researchers, funded mostly by public money, produce knowledge. They give this knowledge — for free — to publishers like Elsevier, Springer, and Wiley. Other researchers, also funded by public money, perform peer review — for free. The publishers then sell access to this knowledge back to the universities that produced it, at prices that have increased far faster than inflation for decades. University libraries, unable to cancel journals without harming their researchers' access to the literature, pay whatever is demanded.

This is textbook rent-seeking: extracting value without creating it, by controlling a bottleneck. The bottleneck is prestige. Researchers need publications in high-impact journals for hiring, tenure, and grants. The journals' prestige is a self-reinforcing equilibrium — they are prestigious because good researchers publish there, and good researchers publish there because they are prestigious. Breaking this cycle requires coordinated action, and coordination is hard when every individual researcher faces a prisoner's dilemma.

The open access movement has made progress, but much of it has been captured by the same publishers. "Gold" open access just shifts the payment from reader to author, with article processing charges that can exceed $10,000. The publishers' margins remain extraordinary — Elsevier's profit margin exceeds 35%, higher than Apple or Google.

The deeper lesson connects to [[Incentive-blindness|incentive blindness]] and [[Markets-as-information-systems|market dysfunction]]. Academics who participate in this system — submitting papers, reviewing for free, serving on editorial boards — are not ignorant of the exploitation. They are trapped by a [[The-curse-of-knowledge|collectively understood]] but individually inescapable incentive structure. The knowledge is not the problem. The coordination failure is.
