# Markets as Information Systems

Hayek's most important insight was not about markets versus planning. It was about information. A price is a signal that aggregates distributed knowledge no single actor possesses. When the price of copper rises, it tells every copper user in the world — simultaneously, without any of them needing to know why — that copper has become relatively scarcer and they should economize on it. No planning committee could replicate this.

But Hayek's insight has been over-applied. Modern advocates treat it as a proof that markets are optimal information systems. They are not. They are the best information systems we have for a particular class of problems — those involving rivalrous goods, excludable benefits, and agents with stable preferences. For public goods, externalities, and situations involving radical uncertainty, markets produce systematically distorted signals.

Climate change is the canonical example. The price of carbon is wrong because the atmosphere is an unpriced commons. Market advocates know this and support carbon taxes — using the state to correct the price signal. But this concession is more radical than they admit. If prices require political correction to reflect true costs, then the distinction between "market information" and "political information" breaks down. Every price encodes political choices about what counts as a cost and who bears it.

This connects to [[Incentive-blindness|incentive blindness]]. People within market systems tend to treat prices as natural facts rather than constructed signals. A startup founder who says "the market validated our idea" is treating revenue as pure information, when it is actually information filtered through existing wealth distributions, regulatory frameworks, and cultural preferences. [[Rent-seeking-in-academia|Academic rent-seeking]] produces similar distortions — citation counts are a market of sorts, and they are just as susceptible to manipulation.
