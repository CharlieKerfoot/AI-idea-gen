# Incentive Blindness

Charlie Munger liked to say "show me the incentives, and I'll show you the outcomes." This is wisdom, but it has a blind spot. People are remarkably bad at seeing the incentives that shape their own behavior. This is not hypocrisy — it is something more interesting. It is a genuine perceptual failure, like a blind spot in vision.

A doctor who receives payments from a pharmaceutical company sincerely believes her prescribing is unaffected. A professor whose tenure depends on publications sincerely believes he pursues truth. A venture capitalist whose fund depends on markups sincerely believes she prices rounds fairly. In each case, the person is not lying. They have simply lost the ability to see how incentives have reshaped their judgment, because the reshaping happened gradually and below the threshold of conscious awareness.

The mechanism is related to [[Epistemology-of-intuition|how intuition works]]. Incentives do not operate by making you consciously decide to act against your principles. They operate by shifting your intuitions — making the incentive-aligned action feel natural, obvious, and correct. The doctor does not think "I should prescribe this drug because the company pays me." She thinks "this drug is genuinely the best option for this patient" — and her genuine belief has been imperceptibly shaped by dinners, conferences, and friendly sales representatives.

This has implications for institutional design. Disclosure requirements — the standard regulatory response — are almost useless, because the problem is not that people are hiding their incentives from others. The problem is that they are hiding their incentives from themselves. Better approaches include structural separation (ban the payments entirely), adversarial processes (require a devil's advocate), and [[Markets-as-information-systems|market mechanisms]] that make the cost of bias visible. But none of these solve the underlying epistemic problem: you cannot see what you cannot see.
