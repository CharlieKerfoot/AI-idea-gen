# Ideas Want to Be Free

Stewart Brand's famous line — "information wants to be free" — is usually quoted as a justification for piracy. But the full quote adds "information also wants to be expensive." The tension between these two forces is where all the interesting questions about intellectual life sit.

Ideas, unlike physical goods, are non-rivalrous. My having an idea does not prevent you from having the same idea. Sharing it does not diminish it — in most cases, sharing makes it stronger, because other minds find applications, objections, and extensions the originator never imagined. The history of science is full of examples: Darwin and Wallace, Newton and Leibniz, the simultaneous invention pattern that suggests ideas are less about individual genius and more about the ecosystem being ready.

But the economic structures we have built around ideas — patents, copyrights, paywalls, trade secrets — treat them as rivalrous. This is not purely irrational. People need incentives to do the work of developing and articulating ideas, and exclusivity is one way to provide those incentives. [[Rent-seeking-in-academia|Academic publishing]] shows what happens when the incentive structure gets captured by intermediaries, but the underlying need for incentives is real.

The deeper issue is that premature formalization kills ideas. When you try to make an idea legible — writing it up, patenting it, turning it into a product — you inevitably narrow it. The messy, generative version that existed in your head and in informal conversation gets replaced by a fixed, bounded version. [[Why-outlines-kill-essays|Outlines kill essays]] for the same reason patents kill inventions: they freeze something that should still be fluid.

The best intellectual cultures — the ones that produce the most [[On-boredom-and-creativity|creative output]] — find ways to keep ideas circulating in their pre-formalized state. Seminars, coffee shops, group chats, working papers. The goal is to maximize the collision rate between half-formed thoughts, because that is where [[Procrastination-as-signal|unexpected connections]] emerge.
