# Why Outlines Kill Essays

The standard writing advice — "start with an outline" — is wrong for essays, and understanding why it is wrong reveals something important about the relationship between writing and thinking.

An outline presupposes that you know what you think before you write. For certain kinds of writing — reports, instruction manuals, grant applications — this is reasonable. The structure of the information is known in advance, and writing is just the act of filling in the cells of a predetermined grid. But an essay, in Montaigne's original sense of the word ("essai" = attempt, trial), is an act of discovery. You write to find out what you think, not to transcribe what you already know.

When you start with an outline, you commit to a structure before you have explored the territory. The outline becomes a cage. Ideas that do not fit the predetermined structure get pruned. Connections that would only emerge through the act of writing never form. The result is a piece that is well-organized but lifeless — it has the shape of thought without the substance.

The alternative is not chaos. It is what I would call exploratory writing: you start with a question or a provocation and follow where it leads. You write badly. You contradict yourself. You discover halfway through that your real argument is something you had not anticipated. Then you revise — often radically, often cutting most of what you wrote. The final product may look like it was written from an outline, but the outline was discovered through writing, not imposed before it.

This connects to [[Epistemology-of-intuition|the role of intuition in thinking]]. The best essays begin with a hunch — a felt sense that something is wrong or interesting. [[Ideas-want-to-be-free|Formalizing too early]] kills the hunch before it can mature into an argument. [[On-boredom-and-creativity|Boredom and open attention]] are prerequisites for the kind of exploratory thinking that produces genuine essays.
