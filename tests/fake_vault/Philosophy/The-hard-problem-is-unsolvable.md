# The Hard Problem Is Unsolvable

David Chalmers' "hard problem of consciousness" — why there is something it is like to be a conscious being, why subjective experience exists at all — is not merely unsolved. I believe it is unsolvable, at least by the methods available to science and analytic philosophy.

Here is the core difficulty. Every scientific explanation works by identifying structural, functional, or dynamical properties that produce an observable phenomenon. But consciousness is not an observable phenomenon in the third-person sense. It is the first-person perspective itself. You cannot explain why first-person experience exists by pointing to more third-person facts, no matter how detailed. This is not a gap in our current knowledge. It is a structural feature of the explanatory framework.

Functionalists try to dissolve the problem by identifying consciousness with functional roles — pain just is whatever plays the pain-role in a cognitive system. But this leaves the zombie thought experiment untouched. A system could play all the right functional roles without any inner experience. You might think zombies are inconceivable, but the burden is on you to show why, and nobody has done this convincingly.

The implications extend beyond philosophy. The [[LLMs-are-not-reasoning|debate about whether LLMs can reason]] is really a special case of the hard problem. We cannot determine from behavioral evidence alone whether a system has inner experience, because behavior is third-person data and experience is first-person. This is not a temporary limitation — it is [[Epistemology-of-intuition|an epistemological wall]] we cannot climb over.

Panpsychism and idealism are attempts to go around the wall rather than over it. I find them increasingly attractive, not because they solve the problem, but because they at least take it seriously.
