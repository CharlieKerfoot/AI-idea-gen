# Free Will as Useful Fiction

I do not believe in libertarian free will. The evidence from neuroscience — Libet experiments, split-brain studies, the predictability of decisions from neural activity before conscious awareness — makes the folk notion of a ghostly "decider" sitting behind your eyes untenable. But I also think hard determinists are making a practical error when they conclude that free will is simply an illusion we should discard.

Free will is better understood as a useful fiction, like money or national borders. It does not correspond to a fundamental feature of reality, but it is indispensable for the social practices that make civilization possible. Criminal law requires it. Promising requires it. Even the act of deliberation — sitting down to think about what you should do — implicitly presupposes that your deliberation can make a difference. Try to hold in mind, while deliberating, that your conclusion is already determined. You cannot do it, or if you can, it degrades the quality of your thinking.

The compatibilist move is to redefine free will as "acting in accordance with your desires without external coercion." This is fine as far as it goes, but it avoids the hard question: why do we feel like agents rather than spectators? [[The-hard-problem-is-unsolvable|The hard problem of consciousness]] applies here too. The phenomenology of choice — the felt sense of an open future — may be the most practically important aspect of consciousness, and we have no good explanation of it.

My pragmatic position is: act as if you have free will, because the fiction is load-bearing. But maintain [[Epistemology-of-intuition|epistemic humility]] about what is actually happening under the hood. The universe may be deterministic, but you still have to decide what to have for lunch.
