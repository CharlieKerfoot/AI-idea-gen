# Epistemology of Intuition

Analytic philosophy has a troubled relationship with intuition. On one hand, philosophical arguments routinely bottom out in appeals to intuition — "intuitively, it seems wrong to push the fat man off the bridge." On the other hand, philosophers are deeply suspicious of intuition as a source of knowledge, because it is unreliable, culturally variable, and resistant to justification.

I think both sides of this tension are correct, and the resolution is to treat intuition not as evidence but as signal. An intuition is like a smoke alarm. It tells you something might be happening that deserves investigation. It does not tell you what is happening, and it can be triggered by toast. But systematically ignoring your smoke alarms is a terrible strategy for staying alive.

In practice, the most productive intellectual work happens at the boundary between intuition and explicit reasoning. You have a hunch that something is wrong with an argument. You cannot yet say what. You sit with the discomfort, poke at it, try to formalize it. Sometimes the intuition dissolves under scrutiny — it was just unfamiliarity, or status quo bias. Sometimes it crystallizes into a genuine objection. The skill of a good thinker is knowing when to trust the alarm and when to override it.

This framework connects to [[The-curse-of-knowledge|the curse of knowledge]] in an interesting way. Expertise strengthens your intuitions, but it also makes them harder to interrogate, because you have forgotten the steps that led to them. The expert's intuition feels like direct perception — "obviously this proof is wrong" — when it is actually compressed reasoning. [[Attention-mechanism-intuition|Transformer attention mechanisms]] may be doing something structurally similar: compressing learned patterns into what functions as artificial intuition.

The upshot: do not trust your intuitions, but do not ignore them either. Use them as search heuristics for where to direct your [[Free-will-as-useful-fiction|deliberate reasoning]].
