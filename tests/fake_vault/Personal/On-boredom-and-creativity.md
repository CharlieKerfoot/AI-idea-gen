# On Boredom and Creativity

I have noticed a pattern in my own creative life: my best ideas come during periods of boredom, and my worst creative droughts coincide with periods of maximum stimulation. This is counterintuitive — you would expect that more input produces more output. But the relationship between information consumption and idea generation is not linear. It is an inverted U.

At the left end of the curve, you have too little input. You need raw material to think with. A person who reads nothing and talks to nobody will not have interesting ideas, because ideas are combinatorial — they emerge from the collision of existing concepts. But at the right end, you have too much input. The mind becomes a consumer rather than a producer. Every moment of potential boredom — waiting in line, sitting on the bus, lying in bed before sleep — gets filled with podcasts, Twitter, news, messages. The background processing that turns raw input into original thought never gets to run.

Boredom is the feeling of your mind having unused capacity. It is uncomfortable precisely because it is productive — the discomfort is the sensation of your unconscious working on problems without your conscious direction. When you eliminate boredom by reaching for your phone, you are interrupting this process. You are trading the possibility of an original thought for the certainty of a prefabricated one.

This is related to [[Procrastination-as-signal|procrastination as signal]]. Sometimes what looks like avoidance is actually incubation. The task you are procrastinating on may need more background processing before your conscious mind is ready to engage with it. [[Why-outlines-kill-essays|Forcing structure too early]] short-circuits this process.

My practical rule: protect at least one hour per day of unstimulated time. No phone, no podcasts, no reading. Walk, shower, stare at the ceiling. Let the [[Epistemology-of-intuition|intuitions]] surface.
